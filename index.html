The software development process is an important procedure used to make software applications. The steps in the software development process include: requirements, design, implementation, verification, and maintenance. In the Requirements Stage, a developer must gather information that helps them determine what problem they need the software to solve. Through speaking to the client, questions such as, “What are you solving?” and “Who is using the product?” can be asked to gain a better understanding and move to the next step of the software development process, the Design Stage. In this stage, choices must be made on how the client’s problem will be solved. A plan for building the software will be formed out of these choices, and includes the software’s structure and user interface. The next stage in the software development process is the Implementation Stage, where the software is implemented according to the design established in the previous stage—this is where the actual programming is done. After this, we have the Testing Stage in which the developer must make sure the software meets the requirements and examine bugs or errors through test cases, which is called validating and verifying. The final step in the software development process is the Installation and Maintenance Stage. In this stage, if the software is a boxed product it can be installed on a user’s system by themselves. Otherwise if the software must be installed for the client on their systems then a service must be provided to install it for the client. Maintenance for the software is also required to make sure that the software functions as it is supposed to by fixing bugs and taking in user feedback.

Software design is important because we live in the digital age, where technology is a significant part of our daily lives. Due to this, user experience is very important and is directly impacted by software design. It ensures that the software is user-friendly, easily navigated, and accessible. It is also important because programming is no longer enough in the field, you must know how to deal with the whole process to succeed.

A software I have used that I think is well designed is Spotify. Music is a very big part of my life, and this streaming platform allows me to discover new music and save my favourite songs very easily due to its simple but effective user interface.

The elements of empathy include:

1. understanding behaviours: what individuals do and why they do it

2. recognising motivations: why individuals make certain choices and decisions

3. considering emotion: empathising with the feelings that individuals have

4. Understanding culture and context: acknowledging that individuals from different backgrounds have varying needs.

The “Sheep” model assumes customers are passive and easily influenced, with little power in making their decisions. The problem with this model is that it underestimates how complex human decision-making is. The “Homo Economicus” model sees customers as rational—making decisions in their own self-interest and looking for the most value with the least cost. This model’s problem is that it doesn’t take into account emotion and culture with decision making. People are not purely rational and have other motivations. The “Tasks and Goals” model looks at improving how efficient or usable a product/service is based on the customers’ tasks and goals. The problem with this is that it doesn’t factor in the variety of customer experiences. It doesn’t take into account emotions, culture, and context which are necessary to understanding customers and how they think.

The “Sheep” model lacks the ‘understanding behaviours’ element by assuming that customers are easily influenced and do not make decisions on their own. This element requires understanding the complex behaviours/individuality of customers. The “Homo Economicus” model lacks the ‘considering emotion’ element by assuming customers only make rational decisions for self-interest. This element requires understanding and empathising with the feelings individuals have that can lead to their decisions. The “Tasks and Goals” model lacks the ‘understanding culture and context’ element by focusing on improving the usability and efficiency of products and services while overlooking the cultural aspects of customer experiences. This element requires acknowledgement that individuals from different backgrounds may have unique perspectives and needs.

Prototyping in software design is a great technique used to create an early, simplified version of a software product before its final development. Prototyping is done by creating simplified models of the software, testing them with users, iterating based on feedback, and using the refined prototype as a guide for final development. Prototyping helps us try out design options and demonstrate concepts for our software, validate requirements, explore solutions, and help identify if the proposed structure fits the users work model. Three key benefits of prototyping include 1. Improving design quality, which leads to better user experiences, cost reduction, faster development, easier maintenance, 2. Reducing development effort, which leads to quicker and more cost-effective software creation, and 3. Improving system usability, which makes it easier for users to navigate and interact with the software, resulting in a more satisfying and efficient user experience. Creating a paper prototype can be done in a few simple steps. First, choose the most important user. select a representative user or who closely reflects the target audience to ensure that the paper prototype is tailored to the needs and preferences of the primary user, making the testing process more relevant and effective. Next, determine some typical tasks. Pinpoint the key actions or interactions that the selected user would perform within the software. These tasks should be essential functionalities and workflows to evaluate how the prototype meets the user's needs and objectives. Then, make screenshots or sketches. Represent the software's interface and functionality using hand-drawn sketches or screenshots. The visual aids serve as the foundation of your paper prototype and should be simple yet clear in conveying the design and interactions. Lastly, perform a usability test. This involves inviting a user to interact with the paper prototype. During the test, provide the user with the defined tasks and scenarios to perform. The main goal is to observe how effectively the user can navigate the prototype, complete tasks, and gather feedback on the user experience. This step is very important for identifying design flaws, usability issues, and opportunities for improvement in the early stages of development.
